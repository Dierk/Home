Fake Human
"You should not anthropomorphize computers." - "Why not?" - "They don't like that!"

We do it all the time

We talk to our pets. Some of us talk to indoor plants. Some even talk to the furniture: "Yes, you [chair] want to be placed at the head of the table".

Computers are made from silicon - like sand and stone. So talking to the computer is like talking to a brick. We rarely expect an answer.

And even though it is obviously silly, we do it anyway. It is not for feedback or because we expect a conversation. It just makes it easier to organize our thoughts, keep the rhythm of our activities and suggest relations. We use the language that we are most familiar with even if it is not very accurate and more on a metaphorical level.

This tendency gets super-boosted with the advent of AI systems that

  • take human language text input and produce human language text output
  • take written context and maintain a conversational state
  • take human voice input and produce human voice output
  • show outcomes that were previously indicative of human skills and behavior

Even for me, when writing this, it is extra effort to avoid phrases like "text comprehension", "talking to", and "achieving results". It should be clear that there is no "comprehension", that there is nobody to "talk to", and there is no innate motivation to "achieve" anything.

Yet, even with all this knowledge, we are so prone to fall for this trap.

My AI does not understand me!

The words that we chose when talking about AI systems reveal our tendency to suggest human characteristics and agency where there isn't

  • Model "training" and machine "learning"
  • Prompting for "chain of thought" and "reasoning"
  • Machine "halluzination"

My goal is not to provide an exhaustive list and joyfully dismantle every single item. You will easily find many more examples. My goal is to raise your awareness.

Is this meant literally or is it just a metaphor? Is it a good one? In which way does machine - let's say "learning" - differ from human "learning" (e.g. humans are aware of what they have learned)? Is it maybe a marketing plot to prey on our tendency to anthropomorphize?

I am immune!

No, you are not.

It is not a matter of awareness or technical knowledge or general education. You can still fall for it.

You will find myriads of reports of well-educated people falling prey to the illusion of having an intelligent conversation with a chatbot. Just a few days ago, a highly sophisticated Math expert showed me his transcript where he tried to "convince" a chatbot of a mathematical proof.

Sigh.

Each single instance of this fallacy is easy to make fun of, but the uncomfortable truth is that we will all fall for it, sooner or later.

I just hope for some good friends to lift me out of the ditch when the time comes. Meanwhile, I extend that service to them.
